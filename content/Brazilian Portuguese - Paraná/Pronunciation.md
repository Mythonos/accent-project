## Consonants

#### Th-fronting 

There is a tendency for the voiceless dental fricative /θ/ to realized as a voiceless labiodental fricative /f/ when found in the middle of a word.

![[../Assets/Tatiana Palm Vowel.wav]]
“I can translate my knowledge now, right, because *every**f**ing* I learned, was in Portuguese. All my work, my profession, was in Portuguese.”

#### /ð/  → \[d] 

Voiced dental fricatives at the start of a word tend to become voiced alveolar plosives. 

![[../Assets/th to d.wav]]
“My accent, it’s like not **d***at* heavy.”
#### /ð/ → \[ɾ]

When found in the middle of a word, the voiced dental fricative may be realized as as an alveolar tap. 
#### /l/ → \[u]

The lateral approximant \[l] when found at the end of a word is usually not fully realized, resulting in something closer to an \[u]. 

![[../Assets/L to W.wav]]

## Vowels

In general, there seems to be a tendency to lengthen vowels, especially for emphatic purposes. 

#### GOAT \[ ɔ̞ ]

The GOAT vowel for General American speakers is usually considered to be a dipthong moving from the close-mid, back vowel \[ɣ] to a somewhat higher and more forward \[ʊ]. For Tatiana, it seems to more likely to be a monopthong, a rounded, mid, back vowel \[ɔ].

![[../Assets/Tatiana Goat Vowel.wav]]
“*So*, when I was *gr**ow**ing* up, I had English in school, but it was like once a week for like forty-five minutes, *so* it’s not something that, we just learned words, you *kn**ow*** like, oh book, pencil, you *kn**ow*** like things like this, not necessarily like make sentences.”

#### PALM \[ɑː]

For Tatiana, the words ‘knowledge’ and ‘all’ fall into the palm lexical set; they are pronounced with an elongated, low, back, unrounded vowel. 

![[../Assets/Palm Vowel.wav]]
“I can translate my *knowledge* now, right? Because everything I learned was in Portuguese. *All* my work, my profession was in Portuguese.”
#### STRUT \[ʌ]

In General American, the strut vowel is more centralized. Contrast that with Tatiana, who pronounces it somewhat lower and more back:

![[../Assets/strut vowel.wav]]
“So I grew *up* in a li, in a little town, in a little city or a town at the coast of Brazil.”

#### FLEECE \[i]

For speakers of Brazilian Portuguese, the long e sound \[iː] appears to lose the length resulting in simply a +high, +front vowel \[i]. There are times in our accent donor's speech where the vowel seems to maintain the length generally found in the American English FLEECE vowel.

![[../Assets/Fleece Vowel.wav]]
“So, uh, I was *reading*, we had the option of *reading* in English, *reading* in French, um or *reading* in Italian so I was always *reading* in Italian, or even French is easier to *read* for a for a Portuguese speaker, for an Italian speaker, because we kinda have from the same root,  but uh so English I was like ahh forget about it.”

#### GOOSE \[ u̞ ]

Words in the GOOSE lexical set have a somewhat lowered version of the close, rounded, and back vowel, \[u].

## Extras

#### Secondary Articulations \[d͡ʒ] and \[t͡ʃ]

Secondary articulations are the result of combining two or three simpler consonants without obscuring the first consonant.
