### The Sound of (Speech) Music
To oversimplify, prosody is the rhythm of speech. It is measured in the three key components of *frequency (pitch), intensity (volume),* and *duration*, which are used to compress pragmatic information for the listener to decode, such as the speaker’s attitude, the meaning of words, or whether or not they have just been asked a question.[^1] Adjustments to frequency, pitch, or duration are used to produce linguistic stress (both at the word and sentence level), rhythm, and intonation, in order to convey the desired meaning.[^2] For example, lexical stress can be used to differentiate *homographs/homophones* (i.e. *subject \[n] vs subject \[v]*) and is especially important in a tonal language where stress is used to determine the meaning of a word.[^x] At the sentence or intonational phrase level, some languages will employ the use of a rising sentence final stress to indicate that a yes-or-no question is being asked. 

### Comparing (Musical) Notes
Some intonation patterns, such as those seen in the image below, point to the general trend over the course of an utterance and are sometimes associated with specific pragmatic functions. For instance, both English and Brazilian Portuguese speakers make use of a rising inflection pattern to signal that they are asking a yes-or-no[^3] question. In addition to these archetypes, speakers, unless they are monotoned, will utilize tonal shifts across what are called *intonation units*. Some languages and dialects exhibit strong intonation patterns with high predictability while others may appear less structured.
