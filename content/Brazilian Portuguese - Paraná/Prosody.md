### The Sound of (Speech) Music

To oversimplify, prosody is the rhythm of speech. It is measured in the three key components of *frequency (pitch), intensity (volume),* and *duration*, which are used to compress pragmatic information for the listener to decode, such as the speaker’s attitude, the meaning of words, or whether or not they have just been asked a question.[^1] Adjustments to frequency, pitch, or duration are used to produce linguistic stress (both at the word and sentence level), rhythm, and intonation, in order to convey the desired meaning.[^2] For example, lexical stress is used to differentiate *homographs* (i.e. *subject \[n] vs subject \[v]*) and is especially important in a tonal language where stress is actually used to determine the meaning of a word.[^x] At the sentence or intonational phrase level, many languages will employ the use of a rising sentence final stress to indicate that a yes-or-no question is being asked.
